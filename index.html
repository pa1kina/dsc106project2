Proposition: CO₂ emissions are still a serious global problem

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • I used a line chart because CO₂ emissions are measured across years, and a line makes the long-term trend easy to follow.
  • I used a filled area under the line to make the overall growth feel larger and more visually persuasive.
  • I added labels for the first and last values so viewers can quickly compare the change from 1995 to 2018.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Persuasive title which pushes the point forward - the selected countries are global leaders and have all reduced their CO2 emissions since 2010.
  • Key with labeled percentage drops to indicate to the reader that the lines haven't just plateaued but decreased as well.
  • The y-axis starts from 0 for the reader to make comparisons between countries and to emphasize the plateaus.

Final Reflection

Working across both visualizations revealed the importance of design choices and how they shape the story of the overall visualization, even if the numbers are identical. The straightforward decisions were adjusting the time range, picking which countries to include or exclude, and adding a filled area under the line to make growth feel larger and more visually persuasive. You can predict the effects of these choices. It was harder to see how natural it feels to make these choices at the time. When making Side A, extending the time range to 1995 really does give a better picture of how emissions have grown, and filling the area under the line makes the overall scale of that growth feel impossible to ignore. When you zoom in on 2010–2018 while building Side B, you can really see a plateau that happened. Neither manipulation felt dishonest while making it, which is exactly what makes deceptive visualization so dangerous. The most surprising thing was how much leaving China out of Side B carried the most weight. Taking out one country, an editorial choice that makes sense on its face since the visualization is framed around "several major economies" instead of "top emitters," changed the story completely. The fact that China is growing so quickly in Side A is what makes that picture so urgent. Without it, the other countries really do look stable, and the percentage drops labeled directly in the legend make the declines feel even more concrete and significant. This taught us that leaving things out is often more powerful than changing them, and it is harder to spot because nothing shown is technically wrong. Changing the wording from "highest emitters" to "leading nations slashing emissions" gave just enough cover to make the exclusion look like a decision about scope rather than a manipulation. This process has made us think of ethical visualization as a promise to protect the reader's right to draw their own conclusions. It is not wrong to try to persuade someone. All design involves framing, and framing is always selective. We would draw the line between choices that direct attention and choices that actively hide important context. Using a filled area to emphasize growth, adding percentage labels to highlight declines, or picking a time range that best supports your argument are all acceptable ways to persuade someone, as long as the visualization does not hide information that a reasonable reader would need to judge the claim. Taking China out of the picture while framing remaining nations as global leaders crossing into progress, or using a filled area to manufacture a sense of alarm, crosses that line because the goal is not to make things clearer but to stop other interpretations. The limit is not about being neutral. It is about whether a reader would feel tricked if they had all the facts.
